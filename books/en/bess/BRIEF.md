# BRIEF — The Last Field

---

## The Premise in One Paragraph

A border collie named Bess has lived ten years on a Yorkshire sheep farm. When her owner dies one morning, and the farm goes up for sale, Bess stays. She watches a daughter arrive from the city to dismantle everything her father built — and understands none of it. Only the smell of the morning, the sound of the gate, and the absence of a voice that always called her name.

---

## Genre and Subgenres

**Primary genre:** Literary fiction
**Subgenres:** Rural elegy, quiet fiction, animal narrator
**Tone:** Dry, observational, restrained. Yorkshire taciturnity carried into prose.

---

## Target Audience

**Age:** 25–50
**Interests:** Readers of Penelope Fitzgerald, Elizabeth Taylor (the writer), Marilynne Robinson. People who have inherited something they didn't know how to keep.
**What they want:** Recognition. A grief that doesn't explain itself.

---

## The Book's Central Question

What do you owe a place — and a creature — that belonged to someone else?

---

## Setting

**Place:** A stone farmhouse in the Yorkshire Dales. Three fields. Dry stone walls. Sheep that need tending.
**Time:** Present day, late October.
**Context:** Henry died at home, in his bed. The farm has been in the family since his father. His daughter Anna lives in Leeds. She is a solicitor. She does not know what to do with sheep.

---

## Main Characters

| Character | Role | One line |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Bess | Protagonist-narrator | Border collie, 10 years. Sees through smell and habit. Doesn't know what "gone" means. |
| Henry | The absent centre | Farmer, 72. Died in his sleep. Present only through what he left. |
| Anna | The inheritor | 35, solicitor, Leeds. Came to close things down. Stayed longer than she planned. |
| Tom | The witness | Farmhand, 58. Has worked here twenty-two years. Arrives every morning. Doesn't know what else to do. |

---

## Structure

**Length:** ~40,000 words, 12–15 chapters
**Narrative:** Third person limited, Bess's PoV
**Time structure:** Linear. Several weeks of late autumn.

---

## What Makes This Book Particular

The narrator — a dog — has no concept of ownership, inheritance, loss, or money. She understands smell, routine, temperature, and presence. The gap between what she perceives and what the reader understands is where the book lives.

---

## Theme and Moral

The agent reads this section before every chapter — the theme must live in the subtext, not in explicit statements.

### Central Theme

Loyalty is not knowing someone has gone.

### Moral

Bess keeps waiting. Tom keeps coming. Anna keeps not leaving. None of them can name what holds them. Something older than a decision.

### What the Book Questions

Is selling the farm the right thing? The book doesn't answer. It only shows what stays in a place after the person who made it leaves.

### Subthemes

- **Routine as memory** → The morning feed, the gate latch, the range stoked at six. When Henry stops, the routine continues without him.
- **Not-knowing as mercy** → Bess doesn't grieve in the human sense. Her limitation is a kind of grace.
- **The land as record** → The farm holds its history in its walls, its walls in its fields. Anna can sell it but can't un-know it.
- **Wordless care** → Tom feeds the sheep without being asked. Anna brings Bess inside out of the rain. Nobody says what it means.

### Anti-Moral

- That Anna is wrong to sell.
- That the countryside is better than the city.
- That dogs understand humans better than humans understand themselves — sentimental and false.
- That Henry's death is a tragedy: it's simply what happened, like October.
- That Bess "saves" Anna. They were in the same place at the same time.

### Physical Anchors of Theme

**The flat cap** — Henry's cap on the hook by the kitchen door. As long as it hangs there, he hasn't entirely gone.

**The feed bucket** — Every morning. First full, then empty, then full in another hand.

**The gate to the top field** — Where Henry always went first. Bess still goes there. Waits.

**The kitchen range** — Always warm. Bess's place. The first thing Anna lights when she arrives. She doesn't know why.

**His voice calling her name** — Not a physical object. The thing Bess waits for longest.
