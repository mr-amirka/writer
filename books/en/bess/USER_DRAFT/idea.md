# Raw Idea — The Last Field

*Author's working notes. Unedited. For agent: read this as source material, not instructions.*

---

Henry died in October. Quietly. In his bed. The way farmers die — without fuss.

Bess was at the foot of the stairs. She'd been there since five in the morning.

I keep thinking about that. The dog who waits. Not grief — she doesn't know what grief is. Just: he usually comes down and today he hasn't. She waits. That's what dogs do.

And then Anna comes. His daughter. She's never been close to the farm. She's a solicitor. She uses words like "estate" and "disposal." She doesn't know what to do with a collie.

But she doesn't leave.

That's the book. Nobody leaves who ought to leave.

---

Tom the farmhand. He's worked here twenty-two years. He just keeps showing up. There's nothing left for him to report to. He doesn't know how to not come.

The farm: Yorkshire Dales. Stone walls. Three fields. Sheep. The kitchen range is always on. That's important. Henry always lit it at five-thirty. It takes Anna three days to figure out how. She lights it.

The flat cap on the hook. As long as it's there it's almost like he is.

---

Style: dry. Short sentences. No sentimentality. Not Brontë — not the moors-and-passion version. More like a man who wouldn't say he was sad but who would drive forty miles in the rain to return a borrowed tool. That kind of Yorkshireness.

POV: Bess. She sees through smell and habit. She doesn't know what death is. She thinks he'll come down eventually. Every morning she goes to the foot of the stairs.

The last image of the book: she stops going to the stairs. She goes somewhere else instead — to Anna, or to the new place, or somewhere. She doesn't know why she stopped. She just did.

---

Question I keep asking: what do you owe a place that wasn't yours to begin with?

Anna didn't ask to inherit it. The farm, the mortgage, the dog.

She sells it in the end. I think. But something stays.

What stays?

That's what I'm writing toward.
