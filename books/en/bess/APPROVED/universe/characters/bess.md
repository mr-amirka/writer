**One line:** A border collie who perceives the world more precisely than any person — and for exactly that reason cannot understand the most important thing that has happened.

---

## Appearance

Mostly black with white chest, white blaze from crown to nose, white tip to tail. Her muzzle has begun greying at the edges. One ear stands; the other folds. She moves low to the ground, as if always about to begin a run she hasn't decided on yet.

## How Bess Knows the World

**First channel — smell.** Every person has a smell. Every mood has a smell. Every change in the farm has a smell. This is how she reads the world.

**Second — sound.** The rhythm of the farm's days. She knows the third stair from the bottom by the pitch of its creak. She knows the gate to the top field by the particular catch of its latch.

**Third — temperature.** Warm = safe. Cold = wrong. She felt it in his hand before she understood any other thing.

**What Bess cannot do:** understand absence as permanent. The word "never" is not available to her. If something is missing, she waits.

## Character

Bess is not sentimental. She is accurate. She does not comfort; she stays. These are different things, and the book is in the difference.

## Arc

| Stage | State |
|-------|-------|
| Opening | World ordered. Smells right. Morning routine intact. |
| Henry's death | The main smell has changed. Bess waits for its return. |
| Anna's arrival | New smell. Not bad — unfamiliar. She observes. |
| End | The unfamiliar smell has become known. She doesn't know when this happened. |

## Speech

Rarely barks. Barks mean something is wrong. All other communication is in her posture, her position in the room, where she goes and where she stays.

## Starting State

**Location:** Foot of the stairs.
**Key relationships:** Henry is not a person separate from the farm. The farm, the routine, Henry — they are one thing. She doesn't know yet that one of them is gone.
