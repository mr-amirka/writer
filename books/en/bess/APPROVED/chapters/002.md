# Chapter 002 — "New Smell" ✦ APPROVED

**Approved from:** `AGENT_DRAFT/chapters/002/v1/content.md`

---

Two weeks is not a long time. Bess did not count days, but she had made her pattern of them: Tom in the morning, the yard, the fields, the bowl filled at seven and again at four. Tom worked without much talking. That was familiar enough. Henry had not talked much either.

The smell in the house was changing. Henry's smell was still there — in the hall carpet, in the weave of the flat cap on its hook — but it was thinning. Each morning she noticed the difference between what it was and what it had been. She had no idea what this meant. She only knew it was happening.

That morning Tom came later than usual. Nine o'clock, not seven. He filled her bowl, stood at the kitchen window for a while, then went to tend the top field. She followed him as far as the gate and then came back.

She was in her place by the range — lit now; Tom had been lighting it every morning — when she heard the car.

She knew cars. She knew Tom's Land Rover three fields away by the pitch of the engine. She knew the van that came for milk, the particular clatter of Henry's old Defender when it made the turn into the lane. This was none of those. This was a smaller engine, coming slowly, as if the driver was uncertain of the lane.

It stopped in the yard.

Bess stood up.

The gate was the sound she heard next — the front gate, not the yard gate. Henry almost never used the front gate. Tom never used it. She heard footsteps on the path, then on the stone of the porch. Then silence. Then the door.

Not the back door. The front door.

The woman came in.

She smelled of a great deal. A wool coat, city, diesel, a soap that was not Henry's soap, coffee that had gone cold somewhere in the car. Bess held still in the kitchen doorway and took it in.

The woman stopped too. They looked at each other from opposite ends of the hall.

"Bess," the woman said.

She said it the way people say a name when they are reminding themselves of it. It was not an invitation. Bess didn't move.

---

Tom came back from the top field at half past ten. He came in through the yard gate, the yard door, the way he always did. He and the woman stood in the kitchen. Bess could hear them from the hall.

They spoke for a while. She could not parse the words — she had learned certain words over ten years, but not these. She heard the tone of them: careful, the way voices went careful when something large was happening. Tom's voice was quieter than usual. The woman's voice went up at the end of sentences.

After a while Tom left.

The woman was alone in the house. Bess heard her going from room to room. Not the way Henry went through rooms — with purpose, with the weight of someone who knew where things were. The woman went slowly. She opened doors and stood in them.

Bess stayed in the hall.

The woman came back to the kitchen. She stood for a moment looking at the hook by the door. The flat cap was on the hook. The woman looked at it for a long time. Then she moved away from it and began opening the cupboards under the sink.

Not the right cupboards.

Bess waited.

The woman went to the sitting room, then came to the foot of the stairs. She stood for a moment, looking up, then did not go. She came back to the kitchen and put the kettle on. The sound of the kettle was familiar — the same kettle, the same click when it finished.

She made tea. She stood at the kitchen window and drank it.

Bess sat in the doorway and watched.

The woman turned round and saw her there. She looked at Bess for a moment.

"I don't actually know what you eat," she said.

She opened the pantry door and stood in front of the shelves. Bess moved to the pantry doorway and sat down. The woman looked at the shelves, then at Bess, then at the shelves again. She reached for one of the tins.

The tin opener was in the drawer by the sink. The woman didn't know this. She tried two drawers before she found it. She opened the tin — the sound of it precise, familiar — and turned the contents into Bess's bowl.

She put the bowl on the floor.

Bess didn't move.

The woman stood for a moment. Then she lowered herself to the floor, slowly, and sat with her back against the kitchen cupboards. She stretched her legs out in front of her and looked at the far wall.

Bess looked at the bowl. At the woman. At the bowl.

She crossed the kitchen and ate.

The woman didn't move. She stayed on the floor while Bess ate. When Bess had finished, she stepped back to the doorway and sat down.

The flat cap was on its hook by the door.

Neither of them moved for a long time.
