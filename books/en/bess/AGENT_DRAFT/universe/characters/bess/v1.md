---

**Full name:** Bess (no surname — she's a dog)
**Age:** 10 years
**Role:** Protagonist-narrator

**One line:** A border collie who understands the farm better than anyone living — and cannot understand that the person who made it make sense is gone.

---

## Appearance

Black and white. White chest, white tip to the tail, white blaze on the face. Her muzzle is beginning to go grey. She moves like a working dog — purposeful, close to the ground. She lies down to watch rather than sitting.

## How Bess Perceives the World

**First channel — smell.** Every person, every mood, every change in the farm — smell first. This is her primary language.

**Second — sound.** She knows every sound the farm makes and what it means. The gate latch. The range. The third stair from the bottom.

**Third — routine.** She knows what time it is by what should be happening. When it doesn't happen, she waits for it to start.

**What Bess cannot do:** understand absence as permanent. If someone is gone, she waits. The word "never" is not in her.

## Character

Bess is not soft. She's a working dog — she has a job, she does it, she's good at it. She isn't affectionate in the way people expect dogs to be. She's precise. She notices. She stays.

That's the point of her.

## Arc

| Stage | State |
|-------|-------|
| Opening | World is ordered. Smell is right. Morning routine holds. |
| Henry's death | Main smell gone. Bess waits for his return. |
| New people | New smells. Not wrong — different. |
| End | One new smell has become familiar. Bess notices — doesn't know why. |

## Speech

Doesn't bark often. When she does — something matters. All other "speech" is position and behaviour.

## Starting State (book opening)

**Location:** Foot of the stairs, farmhouse.
**Key relationships:** Henry = the farm = routine = world. Not a person separate from these things. All one.
