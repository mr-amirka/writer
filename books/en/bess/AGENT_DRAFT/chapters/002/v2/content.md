# Chapter 002 — "New Smell" · v2

*Structural rewrite. Opens at Tom's departure — the transition point — then tracks Anna's arrival as an intrusion into Bess's established pattern. More fragmented time. Contrast between Tom's ease in the space and Anna's foreignness.*

---

Tom came back from the top field at half past ten and said: "She'll be here this afternoon."

He was talking to himself, or to the yard, or perhaps to Henry — he did that sometimes, addressed the air in a way that wasn't quite addressed to anyone. He washed his hands at the yard tap, checked the latch on the gate to the bottom field, gave Bess a brief look that was almost but not quite a question, and then walked back down the lane.

She watched him go from the top of the yard.

Then the farm was just her.

She went inside. Lay in her place by the range. Tom had been lighting it every morning for two weeks; she had stopped expecting that the footsteps on the stairs would follow. She had not stopped listening for them.

---

The car came at two in the afternoon. She heard it while it was still in the lane — an engine she didn't know, slower than it should have been, making the turn into the yard with the particular hesitation of someone who had never driven this lane before.

It stopped.

Then the front gate.

The woman came in through the front of the house. Henry didn't use the front. Tom didn't use the front. Everyone Bess had known came round the back and in through the yard.

She stood in the kitchen and listened to the front door open.

The smell came first. A coat, city, soap, the cold from inside a car. Then footsteps on the hall stone. Then the woman appeared in the kitchen doorway.

She was not Henry. She was not Tom. She did not smell of the lane or the fields or livestock or the inside of the house. She smelled of somewhere else.

They looked at each other.

"Bess," the woman said. Like a reminder to herself.

Bess didn't move.

The woman looked around the kitchen. At the range. At the window over the sink. At the table. At the hook by the door where the flat cap was. She looked at the flat cap for a moment and then away.

She put her bag on the table.

---

She was in the house all afternoon. Bess learned the sound of her: shoes on stone, shoes on the wooden stairs — she went upstairs in shoes — the sound of doors opening and closing, drawers. The woman was learning the house the hard way, the way people learned things who hadn't been there before.

Bess stayed in the hall. She knew the rooms. She was not needed to navigate them.

At five o'clock the woman came back to the kitchen and stood looking at the cupboards. She opened several of the wrong ones. Under the sink. The one with the mop. The one with Henry's account books that no one had opened in months.

Bess went to the pantry door and stood beside it.

The woman noticed. She opened the pantry.

She stood looking at the shelves — tins, dried things, bottles. She reached for one of the dog tins. Then she turned and looked around the kitchen, opening drawers. Bess waited. The woman found the tin opener in the drawer by the sink.

She opened the tin. Turned it into the bowl. Put the bowl on the stone floor.

Then she sat down. Just sat on the floor, against the cupboards, legs out in front.

She looked at the far wall.

Bess looked at the bowl.

She went to it and ate.

The woman sat on the floor while she did. When Bess had finished she went to the kitchen doorway and sat. The woman was still there. The flat cap was on its hook. The range ticked as it cooled toward evening.

No one spoke.
