Before five. Dark.

She is at the foot of the stairs. She is always at the foot of the stairs before five. The stone is cold. The hall smells of him — the wax jacket on the hook, the tobacco, the wool of things worn a long time.

She waits.

The light changes under the kitchen door. Grey first. Then pale. These are the colours of time passing. Grey means the sheep are moving. Pale means the sun.

He does not come.

She goes to the kitchen door and stands against it. Cold on the other side. The range has gone out — she knows this from the absence of the smell, the hot-iron smell that means it's burning. He always lights it at five-thirty. He has lit it every morning she has known.

She goes back to the stairs.

She waits.

The smell is wrong. Not wrong like danger — wrong like something has shifted that she cannot find. His smell is everywhere in the house: the stairs, the hall, the kitchen. But it is the kind of smell that doesn't move. A smell that stays in one place.

She goes up.

The bedroom. She almost never comes here. The door is not closed all the way. She pushes it.

He is in the bed. She goes to him. His hand. Cold.

She lies down.

---

Later: Tom's voice downstairs.

Then steps on the stairs.

Then quiet.

Then more people, more voices, more steps she doesn't know. She goes to the kitchen. She lies under his cap.

The cap smells of him.

She lies there.

Outside, a car she doesn't know starts and leaves. Then another. Then it is quiet.

The bowl is empty.

She waits beside it.
