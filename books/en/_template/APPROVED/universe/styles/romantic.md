# Style: Romantic

Alternative narrative voice. Creates a separate RELEASE in `RELEASES/romantic/`.
Based on `styles/main.md`, but radically changes the delivery.

**Principle:** The same plot, the same facts — a different way of feeling the story.
The style doesn't rewrite events. It changes how they're refracted through the narrator.

---

## Voice and Tone

**Distance:** Close — the narrator sympathises and is involved
**Emotionality:** Open — interior states described directly
**Irony:** Absent or gentle

Voice: lyrical, unhurried, notices beauty where the main style notices only facts.

---

## Vocabulary Register

**General register:** Literary, at times poetic
**Emphasis on:** Synesthesia, body and nature metaphors, soft images
**Avoids:** Harsh verbs, technical vocabulary, slang

**Words frequently used:**
quietly, light, touch, breath, the space between, almost, still

**Words never used:**
grabbed, struck, lunged (replace with softer synonyms or sensory descriptions)

---

## Sentence Rhythm

**Typical length:** Medium and long, with nested subordinate clauses
**Structure:** Smooth periods, anaphora, repetition for emphasis
**Pace:** Deliberately slower than the main style

---

## Emphases

**Described in detail:** Emotions, physical sensations, space as reflection of inner life
**Omitted:** Technical details, logistics of actions

---

## Transformation Examples

### Action scene
> Main: "He ran down the street."
> Romantic: "He walked faster than he needed to — as though something was chasing him that he couldn't name."

### Emotional scene
> Main: "She felt afraid."
> Romantic: "Something tightened in her somewhere between her throat and her heart — somewhere you can't reach with your hands."

### Meeting description
> Main: "They met at a café."
> Romantic: "The café smelled of cinnamon and other people's conversations. She sat by the window — the only one not watching the street."

---

## Dialogue

Lines are shorter than in the main style. More pauses, more description of what's left unsaid.
Beats describe sensations, not actions.

> Instead of: "Get out," he said."
> Romantic: ""Get out." His voice was even. She didn't move."
