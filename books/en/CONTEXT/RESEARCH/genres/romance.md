## The Genre Contract — The Most Important Thing to Understand

Romance is the only genre with an explicit promise about the ending.

The reader opens the book already knowing: there will be a happy ending. This is not a spoiler — it's the genre contract. Breaking it means betraying the reader. Tension in a romance doesn't come from "will they be together?" but from "HOW — and what will they have to go through?"

Two permissible endings:
- **HEA** (Happily Ever After) — the couple is together, the future is clear, the relationship is established
- **HFN** (Happy For Now) — together now, the future is open, but the moment is happy

Everything else is tragedy or a different genre.

---

## The Central Mechanic: Attraction + Obstacle = Tension

Without both components there's no story.

**Attraction without obstacle** → they got together in chapter one and are happy. Nothing to read.

**Obstacle without attraction** → they suffer, but we don't care. Doesn't work.

The author's job — hold both poles simultaneously for as long as possible. Every chapter must simultaneously move the characters closer together and create new distance.

## Conflict: External vs. Internal

This is the most important decision when building a romance.

### External conflict (weak on its own)
Circumstances that stand in the way: families are enemies, one is leaving, one has a fiancée. Works as a catalyst but isn't enough as a foundation. When the circumstance disappears — so does the conflict.

### Internal conflict (strong)
The character's own fears, wounds, and beliefs that prevent them from opening up. This isn't circumstance — they themselves are standing in their own way.

Examples of internal barriers:
- "I'm not worthy of love" (wound: past rejection)
- "Love is losing control, and control means survival" (wound: chaotic childhood)
- "If I get attached — I'll inevitably lose" (wound: losing someone close)
- "I must be strong because people are watching" (wound: responsibility for others)

**Golden rule:** the best conflict — when precisely what makes two characters perfect for each other is what they fear most.

## The Structure of a Romance

Romance uses the three-act structure, but with distinctive turning points.

```
Act I               Act II-A            Act II-B           Act III
Meeting             Closeness           Separation         Reunion
(10-15%)            (15-50%)            (50-75%)           (75-100%)
    ↓                    ↓                   ↓                   ↓
First contact      Growing             Dark Moment        Breakthrough → HEA
Attraction begins  intimacy            Everything seems   Both change
Conflict defined   + conflicts         over               Together
```

### Key Points

**Meeting (Meet-cute or non-meet-cute)** — ~10%
Doesn't have to be sweet and funny. Can be a collision, dislike, indifference. One thing matters: something caught — even if the character denies it.

**First turning point** — ~25%
Circumstances force them to interact against their will. The voluntary choice to avoid it is gone.

**Midpoint: false closeness** — ~50%
They're almost together. It seems like it'll work out. This is the most dangerous moment — where the reader relaxes. The illusion must be destroyed immediately.

**Dark Moment** — ~70-75%
The darkest point of the book. The relationship seems utterly destroyed — and for reasons that come from within the characters themselves, not external circumstances. Must be internally motivated: one of them did (or didn't do) something in accordance with their deepest fear.

Requirements for the Dark Moment:
- The reader must understand WHY the character did it (even if it was wrong)
- It must feel impossible to fix
- The cause — internal barrier, not misunderstanding

**Breakthrough** — ~85%
The character recognises their fear and chooses love despite it. Not a coincidence — an active decision. The most vulnerable and most important action in the book.

**HEA** — ~95-100%
Declaration, reunion, new equilibrium. Don't drag it out. After the breakthrough — the reader wants to exhale, not relive everything.

## Characters: Two Requirements

### They Must Be Worthy of Each Other

The reader must understand what specifically about this person makes the protagonist better — smarter, braver, more themselves. Not "he's handsome" or "she's kind." What exactly in the partner unlocks something blocked in the protagonist?

### Each Must Have Their Own Arc

The romantic relationship is a catalyst for personal growth, not its replacement. By the end of the book each character must be a different person — not because the partner "fixed" them, but because the relationship gave them the safety or challenge needed for growth.

**Red flag:** if one character exists only to save/love/support the other — that's not an arc, that's a function.

## Chemistry on the Page — How It's Created

Physical attraction is easy to write. Chemistry is hard. The difference:

**Attraction:** "she was beautiful, and he noticed"

**Chemistry:** they argue about something insignificant, and in the middle of the argument one suddenly realises the other is the only person who actually understood the point. This frightens them.

Chemistry is built on:

1. **Mutual recognition** — "you're like me in what I'm ashamed of"
2. **Intellectual equality** — they can talk, argue, surprise each other
3. **Accidental vulnerability** — one said something more honest than they intended, and the other noticed
4. **Humour and play** — they can make each other laugh; laughter = trust
5. **Irritation as a mirror** — "why does he irritate me so much?" often means "he sees through me"

## Point of View (PoV): The Key Choice

### Single PoV
Classic. The reader knows only what the protagonist knows. The mystery of the second character lasts longer. Risk: the second character may seem flat.

### Dual PoV (contemporary standard)
The reader sees both from the inside. This creates a particular tension: we see how both want each other and both are afraid — and we cannot simply shout at them "just talk to each other already."

Rule of dual PoV: each character's chapter must be written so differently that the reader knows the PoV without the heading. Different vocabulary, different attention focus, different interior monologue.

### What to Avoid
Switching PoV mid-scene — almost always a mistake. The reader loses their anchor.

## Tropes: Why They Work and How to Use Them

A trope is not a cliché. It's a pre-built tension structure with mechanics the reader already understands. A smart author takes the trope and fills it with fresh characters.

| Trope | Built-in tension mechanic |
|-------|--------------------------|
| Enemies to lovers | Barrier = active dislike; every closeness is self-overcoming |
| Friends to lovers | Barrier = fear of losing the friendship; the stakes are enormous |
| Second chance | Barrier = past wound; must not just love but also forgive |
| Forced proximity | Barrier = circumstances will end; must confess before they do |
| Forbidden love | Barrier = external rules; choice between love and duty |
| Marriage of convenience | Barrier = artificiality; when "fake" becomes real |
| Grumpy/sunshine | Barrier = difference; sunshine opens grumpy, grumpy protects sunshine |

**How to work with a trope:**
1. Name the trope explicitly to yourself
2. Define its built-in tension mechanic
3. Find where the specific characters' natures make this trope unpredictable

## Slow Burn vs. Instant Attraction

**Slow burn:**
Characters don't acknowledge the attraction for a long time — or acknowledge it but don't act. Tension accumulates through indirect signs: accidental touch, a gaze held a second too long, one action that "doesn't fit the theory." Requires discipline: can't give easy releases.

**Instant attraction:**
They feel each other immediately — and precisely that is frightening. Tension is built not on "when will they admit it" but on "why do they keep getting in their own way." Harder — needs a very strong internal conflict.

Both work. The choice depends on the characters, not genre preferences.

## Intimacy Scenes: Function, Not Decoration

A physical intimacy scene in a good romance does plot work:
- Changes the balance of the relationship (before and after — different people in different positions)
- Reveals vulnerability the character was hiding
- Creates a new stake (now there's something to lose)
- Mirrors the internal conflict through the physical (one controls — the other lets go of control)

If the scene can be removed without damaging the plot — it shouldn't exist.

Level of explicitness — a stylistic decision, not a genre obligation. A literary romance can be chaste and still erotically charged through subtext.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### "Insta-love"
They met and immediately fell in love. No arc, no growth, no tension.
→ Replace with: instant attraction (physical or intellectual) + a long road to trust

### Conflict Solved by a Conversation
"If they just talked, it would all be resolved." This means the conflict is superficial — it's about misunderstanding, not fear.
→ Replace with: a conflict where conversation is impossible until the character has changed internally

### The "Perfect" Character
Beautiful, smart, kind, successful — and no real flaws. The reader has nothing to identify with.
→ Replace with: flaws that flow from the character's central wound

### Passive Protagonist
Love "happens" to them. They react but don't act.
→ Replace with: in every scene the protagonist makes a choice — even small, even wrong

### Dissolution into the Partner
By the end of the book one character has lost their own identity for the sake of the relationship.
→ Check: do both characters have a life, goals, and values independent of the relationship?

## Size and Pace

Romance is typically shorter than literary prose:

| Type | Words | Chapters | Chapter size |
|------|-------|----------|-------------|
| Novella | 20,000–40,000 | 8–15 | 2,000–3,500 words |
| Standard novel | 50,000–90,000 | 20–35 | 2,000–4,000 words |
| Long novel | 90,000–120,000 | 30–45 | 2,500–4,000 words |

**Pace:** romance is typically faster than literary prose. Long descriptive pauses kill tension. Every scene must move either the plot or the relationship.

## What Distinguishes Good Romance from Bad

Bad romance: two beautiful people overcome external circumstances and unite.

Good romance: two complex people, each fearing precisely what the other can give — slowly, with mistakes and setbacks, choose each other despite themselves. And that choice changes them both.

The theme of a romance is never "they found each other." The theme is always — what love specifically required of each of them, and what they were willing to do for it.
