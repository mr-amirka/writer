# Narrative Structure — Core Principles

Global research, applicable to all books in the `en` domain.

---

## Three-Act Structure

```
Act I (25%)     → Act II (50%)       → Act III (25%)
Setup           → Rising action       → Climax / Resolution
```

Plot points:
- **Inciting incident** (~10%) — the world changes, the hero is forced to act
- **First turning point** (~25%) — the hero crosses the threshold, no turning back
- **Midpoint** (~50%) — false victory or defeat, stakes double
- **Second turning point** (~75%) — everything falls apart, the dark night of the soul
- **Climax** (~90%) — the final confrontation
- **Resolution** (~95-100%) — the new world

---

## Chapter Structure

Each chapter is a mini-story with its own arc:
1. **Anchor** — where we are, with whom, what's happening (no info-dump exposition)
2. **Character goal** — what they want in this scene
3. **Conflict** — what stands in the way
4. **Reversal** — what changed (not necessarily in the hero's favour)
5. **Hook** — why read the next chapter

If a chapter lacks even one of these elements — it sags.

---

## Pace

- **Slow pace:** long sentences, description, interior monologue, dialogue with pauses
- **Fast pace:** short sentences, action, dialogue without beats, skipped description

Rule: the climactic moment of a scene — the shortest sentences in the book.

---

## Point of View (PoV)

| PoV | Effect | Risk |
|-----|--------|------|
| First person | Intimacy, unreliability | Limited scope |
| Third limited | Flexibility + closeness | Temptation to break the limit |
| Third omniscient | Scale | Distance from characters |
| Second person | Engagement, strangeness | Tires quickly |

Switching PoV mid-scene — almost always a mistake.

---

## Dialogue

Functions of dialogue (at least two of three in each exchange):
1. Advances the plot
2. Reveals character
3. Creates subtext (character says one thing, means another)

Signs of weak dialogue:
- "As you know, Bob..." (exposition in dialogue)
- All lines roughly the same length
- Characters address each other by name more than once per scene

---

## Show, Don't Tell — With Caveats

"Show" works for emotion and character.
"Tell" is acceptable for:
- Quick transitions through uninteresting periods of time
- Background information that doesn't need emphasis
- Deliberate distancing (the cold effect)

Slavishly following "show don't tell" bloats the text.
