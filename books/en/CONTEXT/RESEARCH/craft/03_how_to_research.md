# How to Conduct Analysis in a Writing Project

Related files: `craft/01_structure.md`, `craft/02_chapter_size.md`, `genres/romance.md`

---

## The Core Rule: Analysis Starts with Data, Not Opinions

The typical mistake: the author (or agent) writes a "study" from what they already know.
That's not research — it's systematising pre-existing biases.

**Real analysis = fresh data → processing → conclusions**

```
Data Collection      Processing               Conclusions
(external sources)   (filtering, clusters)    (for this specific project)
      ↓                    ↓                       ↓
Current market      Patterns & anomalies     What THIS book should do
Reader reviews      Contradictions between   What to avoid
Academic data        sources                 Where the risk is, where the opportunity
Competitive         Gaps in data
analysis
```

Analysis based only on training data is analysis of the past. Markets, readers, and genres change faster than language models are updated.

## Data Sources for Writing Analysis

### Primary (fresh, priority)

| Source | What it gives | How to access |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| NPD BookScan / Circana | Real sales by genre and format | Public reports, press releases |
| Goodreads / StoryGraph | Reader patterns, ratings, reading pace | Open data, genre search |
| Amazon bestseller lists | What's being bought right now | Public lists |
| BookTok / TikTok #booktok | What's going viral and why | Browse hashtags |
| Literary agent blogs | What agents are looking for | QueryTracker, Publisher's Marketplace |
| Publishers Weekly / Shelf Awareness | Market trends with data | Open articles |

### Secondary (processed, useful)

| Source | What it gives |
|--------|--------------|
| Academic research (arXiv, JSTOR) | Reader psychology, structural analysis |
| Bestseller analysis (retrospective) | Patterns in successful books |
| Author interviews (Publishers Weekly, Shelf Awareness) | How authors make decisions |
| Critic reviews (NYT Book Review, The Guardian) | What counts as quality |

### Tertiary (use with caution)

- Blog advice without source links → opinion, not fact
- "Rules" without citations → tradition, not law
- Personal reader experience → anecdote, not pattern

## Analysis Criteria and Slices for Writing

Any analysis for a book project should cover several of these slices. No single slice alone gives a complete picture.

---

### Slice 1: Market Analysis

**Question:** What are people reading and buying right now?

**Metrics:**
- Sales volume by genre (units, dollars)
- Genre growth / decline rate year over year
- Formats (e-book, print, audio)
- Price ranges
- Distribution channels (stores, online, subscriptions)

**Current data (2024–2025):**
- Romance: +24% year-over-year in print sales, 51 million units over 12 months
- Romantasy (romance + fantasy): $610 million in 2024 (+34% from 2023) — fastest-growing subgenre
- Rebecca Yarros "Onyx Storm": 2.7 million copies in the first week — adult fiction record in 20 years
- Dark romance: triple-digit growth — themes of power dynamics, morally grey characters
- Mass-market paperback: collapse (131 million → 21 million units from 2004 to 2024)
- BookTok (TikTok): primary sales driver for Gen Z and millennials
- Genre boundaries: 45% of top thrillers and literary fiction now contain romantic elements

**Conclusions for authors:** Pure genres are blurring. Hybrids sell. Dark themes no longer kill romance — they amplify it.

---

### Slice 2: Reader Psychology

**Question:** What happens in the reader's mind while reading and why do they continue?

**Metrics:**
- Immersion level (Transportation Theory, Gerrig 1993)
- Empathic response
- Drop-off rate (where readers abandon the book)
- Emotional memory after reading

**Research data:**
- Transportation Theory — the key model: the more fully a reader is "transported" into the world of a book, the stronger the change in beliefs and emotions after reading
- Readers of literary fiction show significantly higher empathy scores (Mar et al., 2009, York University)
- Eye-tracking study (arXiv 2306.04043): reader engagement in literary prose is higher with high density of concrete details and low density of abstractions
- Engagement is unpredictably individual: genre preference explains less variance than individual reader traits

**Conclusions for authors:** Concrete details → more immersion. Abstractions → exit from transportation. Empathy with a character is built through psychological complexity, not likability.

---

### Slice 3: Structural Analysis

**Question:** How are stories that work actually structured?

**Major models:**

| Model | Author | Focus | Best for |
|-------|--------|-------|---------|
| Hero's Journey (Monomyth) | Campbell | Character transformation | Fantasy, epic |
| Save the Cat | Snyder | 15 precise beats, pacing | Commercial prose, screenwriting |
| Story Grid | Coyne | 5 commandments of genre | Genre accuracy |
| Three-Act | Aristotle → Hollywood | Basic arc | Universal foundation |
| Snowflake | Ingermanson | Scaling from idea | Planning |

**Key finding from comparison:** All models describe the same thing in different words. Choosing a model is a question of working vocabulary, not truth. Useful to know 2–3 for triangulation.

**What algorithmic bestseller analysis revealed (Goodreads study, 2018):**
- Popular books use "everyday language" with high verb-character alignment
- Fiction authors have a higher repeat success rate than nonfiction authors
- An algorithm can predict popularity by combining topic/plot/character/setting — but cannot predict awards, adaptations, or the celebrity effect

---

### Slice 4: Competitive Analysis (comparable titles)

**Question:** Which specific books are direct competitors? What works in them?

**Metrics:**
- Goodreads rating + number of ratings
- Average book length
- PoV (who tells the story)
- Pacing (short chapters vs. long)
- Tone (dark/light, ironic/serious)
- What readers praise (analysis of top reviews)
- What they criticise (analysis of 2-3 star reviews)

**How to conduct:**
1. Choose 5–8 books with similar theme/genre/audience
2. Read 20–30 reader reviews for each (not critics — readers)
3. Note patterns: what repeats in praise, what repeats in criticism
4. Build a table: what "job" does each book do for the reader (escapism / catharsis / recognition / information / entertainment)
5. Find unoccupied spaces

---

### Slice 5: Prose Analysis

**Question:** How is the text itself structured at the level of sentences, paragraphs, chapters?

**Metrics:**
- Average sentence length (and variance)
- Dialogue-to-prose ratio
- Density of abstractions vs. concrete details
- Verb activity (active vs. passive voice)
- Rhythm of chapter endings (what do 10 chapters end on in a row?)
- Frequency of PoV changes and their mechanics

**How to analyse:**
Take a favourite book in the genre. Write out the first 10 sentences from each of 5 random chapters. Count: how many words per sentence? How many concrete nouns? How many abstract ones? This gives a "prose fingerprint" — which can be compared against your own text.

---

### Slice 6: Thematic Analysis

**Question:** What does the book say about the world? Why does this matter right now?

**Metrics:**
- Central value conflict (what is fighting what?)
- Social context (why is this theme relevant this year?)
- Subtexts (what's not said explicitly but present)
- Reader identification (which groups does it resonate with?)

**Example: why dark romance is growing in 2024–2025:**
- Readers (predominantly women) find in "dark" stories a safe space to explore power, control, fear
- BookTok created communities around "guilty pleasure" — the stigma disappeared
- Romance as antidote to economic instability (historically romance sales rise during crises)

---

### Slice 7: Temporal Analysis (trends vs. permanent)

**Question:** What in this genre is timeless, and what is this season's fashion?

**Permanent in romance:**
- HEA/HFN as genre contract — will never change
- Central love story as the main plot line
- Emotional transformation of both characters

**Trending (may change in 2–3 years):**
- Romantasy as a subgenre — likely to saturate
- Dark romance as a format — cyclical, comes and goes
- BookTok as a channel — will be displaced by the next platform

**Conclusion:** A book written for a trend comes out when the trend has already peaked (development cycle 1–3 years). Better to build on a permanent foundation, use a trend as tone or decoration.

---

### Slice 8: Cultural and Market Context

**Question:** How does this genre work in the English-language context? What are the platform and distribution specifics?

**What to check:**
- Are there English-language authors in this genre with large audiences? (If not — opportunity or absent market)
- Which themes and images work for English-language readers but not for others — and vice versa
- How does debut literary fiction land differently from genre work
- Distribution platform specifics (Amazon KDP, IngramSpark, Kindle Unlimited, Audible, Kobo)
- Agent query process (Publishers Marketplace, QueryTracker) vs. self-publishing

## How to Build an Analysis: Step-by-Step Process

```
Step 1: QUESTION
  Formulate a specific question that needs an answer.
  Bad: "research romance"
  Good: "What word count and chapter pace do romantasy readers expect in 2024?"

Step 2: SOURCES
  Identify 3–5 sources with current data.
  At least 1 source with numbers (market, ratings, research).

Step 3: COLLECTION
  Gather data. Don't interpret — only facts.
  Record collection date (data gets stale).

Step 4: CONFLICTS
  Find contradictions between sources. These matter more than agreement.
  Contradiction = either methodological difference, or genuine uncertainty.

Step 5: PATTERNS
  What repeats in 3+ sources? That's probably true.
  What appears in only 1 source? Needs verification.

Step 6: PROJECT CONCLUSIONS
  Translate into specific recommendations for the current book.
  Not "romance is popular now," but "for this book that means X."
```

---

## Red Flags of Bad Analysis

- **No sources** — that's opinion, not analysis
- **Sources older than 3 years** without checking currency — market changes fast
- **No numbers** — qualitative analysis without quantitative is incomplete
- **No contradictions** — means the sources aren't different, just the same thing reformulated
- **Conclusion matches initial belief** — analysis didn't change the view → possible confirmation bias
- **"Always" and "never"** — in writing there are no absolute rules, only patterns

## Application to This Project

For each new book it's recommended to conduct analysis across at least 4 slices before writing:

| Slice | Minimum work |
|-------|-------------|
| Market | Check current top-20 of the genre on Amazon/Goodreads, find 1 market report |
| Reader | Read 30–50 reader reviews (not critics) on 3–5 competitor books |
| Competitive | Table of 5 comparable books with parameters |
| Prose | Break down 3 chapters from the best book in the genre at sentence level |

Results — in `RESEARCH/` for the book (not in the global CONTEXT, if specific).
Global findings (applicable to all books) — in `CONTEXT/RESEARCH/`.
